Tokyo Travel Guide

Plan Tokyo by neighborhood, not by random attractions.

Tokyo gets overwhelming when travelers treat the city like a single cluster. The smarter move is to build each day around one area, one energy level, and one clear theme. That is how you get a trip that feels exciting instead of exhausting.

Where to stay in Tokyo

For first-time visitors, the best base depends on how you want the city to feel. Shinjuku is efficient and energetic, with strong transport connections and late-night food. Ueno is simpler and slightly calmer, which makes it good for families or older travelers. Ginza and Tokyo Station feel polished and convenient, especially for travelers who want smoother hotel quality and direct airport access.

How to group your days

The easiest way to avoid transit fatigue is to divide Tokyo into zones. East Tokyo works well for Asakusa, Ueno, and Skytree. Central Tokyo fits Ginza, Marunouchi, and nearby museums. West Tokyo works for Shibuya, Harajuku, Omotesando, and Shinjuku. A slower local-feeling day can focus on Kiyosumi Shirakawa, Shimokitazawa, or Nakameguro.

  • Day 1: traditional Tokyo and first-night food
  • Day 2: central shopping, design, and a polished evening
  • Day 3: trend districts and cafe-heavy wandering
  • Day 4: slower neighborhood day or half-day add-on

Tokyo food zones matter

Tokyo rewards travelers who eat by district instead of by random viral lists. Tsukiji and Toyosu help if you want seafood and market energy. Shinjuku and Shibuya are dense and high-choice. Kappabashi is great if you love kitchenware and food culture. Shimokitazawa and Nakameguro are stronger for cafes than for major sightseeing, which makes them perfect for a lighter day.

Pacing for first-time travelers

The biggest Tokyo mistake is stacking too many major districts into one day. Even if the subway is efficient, station transfers, exits, crowds, and decision fatigue add up fast. Two anchor areas a day is usually enough. Three works only if one stop is lighter and more flexible. That is especially true for travelers with seniors, kids, or mobility needs.
